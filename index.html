June 2024 Bernoulli Research Research / Derivation
Research — Hand Derivation

Problem Statement

Could Calculus II techniques be used to model how pressure changes as an incompressible fluid flows through a channel with complex, continuously varying wall geometry? Rather than assuming simple straight walls, the goal was to define the channel boundaries as arbitrary smooth functions — then use integration and Bernoulli's equation to resolve the full pressure and velocity field at every cross-section downstream.

Mathematical Formulation

The upper and lower channel walls are defined as continuous functions P(x) and Q(x). The cross-sectional area at any position x is found by integrating the gap between them:

A(x) = ∫ [ P(x) − Q(x) ] dx

For validation, parabolic test walls were chosen — P(x) = 0.001x² + 20 and Q(x) = −0.001x² + 10 — producing a diverging channel whose area grows with x. With area defined at every slice, conservation of mass (continuity) gives velocity:

Q = A₁V₁ = A₂V₂ → V(x) = Q / A(x)

As the channel widens, velocity drops. That velocity is then substituted into Bernoulli's equation to recover the pressure at each cross-section:

P₁ + ½ρV₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρV₂² + ρgh₂

The domain is discretized into 100 slices (n = 0 → 99). At each slice the solver computes area, solves for velocity, then solves for pressure — marching downstream until the full field is resolved.

Project — Python Implementation

A Python script that takes any two user-defined wall functions P(x) and Q(x), discretizes the channel into 100 cross-sectional slices, and marches downstream — applying continuity to solve for velocity at each slice, then Bernoulli's equation to recover pressure. Outputs four plots: the raw channel geometry, velocity as a function of x, the channel shaded by velocity gradient, and the channel shaded by pressure gradient — giving a full visual picture of how the flow field evolves through any smooth geometry.

December 2024 Potential Flow — Early Research Research / Derivation
Research — Theory & Derivation

What I Was Researching

Potential flow is a model of ideal fluid flow where the flow is assumed to be irrotational and incompressible. The velocity field is derived from a scalar potential function φ, and the entire flow is governed by Laplace's equation:

∇²φ = 0

Because this equation is linear, elementary flows — uniform streams, sources, sinks, and doublets — can be superimposed to build up complex flow fields. The central goal of this research was understanding how to combine a uniform stream with a doublet to analytically model potential flow around a cylinder, producing the velocity and pressure distribution over the entire surface.

January 2025 Potential Flow — Continuation Research / Derivation
Research — Continuation

Revisiting the governing PDEs from scratch — establishing Laplace's equation as the foundation for potential flow and deriving the doublet flow solution used to model a cylinder. Introduces the superposition principle and uniform flow boundary conditions.

Derivation of line sources and sinks, and how superimposing them with a uniform stream produces half-body and Rankine oval geometries — showing that complex body shapes can be constructed purely through flow superposition.

Extends the cylinder solution to include circulation — adding a vortex term that breaks the symmetry of the flow field and generates lift. Covers the Kutta-Joukowski theorem connecting circulation directly to lift force.

Final pages covering the Joukowski conformal transformation — a complex mapping that transforms a cylinder into an airfoil-like shape, allowing potential flow solutions around a cylinder to be directly applied to lifting bodies.

Problem Statement

After understanding the theory, the goal became: could multiple sources and sinks be strategically placed and superimposed to construct complex, arbitrary closed body shapes — and then solve for the full velocity and pressure field around them? Rather than being limited to a cylinder, the idea was to use superposition of many elementary flows to define the geometry itself, turning potential flow into a flexible tool for modeling flow around any shape built from a distribution of singularities.
