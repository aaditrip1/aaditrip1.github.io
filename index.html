2024 Bernoulli Research Research / Derivation
Research — Hand Derivation

Before writing a single line of code, the governing physics were derived by hand. Starting from the continuity equation and Bernoulli's principle, the problem was reformulated around arbitrary wall geometry — replacing fixed straight walls with continuous functions, allowing any smooth channel shape to be analyzed.

Mathematical Formulation

The goal was to simulate how fluid velocity and pressure change as flow moves through a channel with complex, varying wall geometry. Instead of assuming simple straight walls, the channel boundaries are defined as mathematical functions, allowing the solver to handle any smooth shape — making it directly applicable to real engineering geometries like rocket nozzles and jet engine inlets.

The upper and lower boundaries are defined as continuous functions P(x) and Q(x). The cross-sectional area at any position x is:

A(x) = ∫ [ P(x) − Q(x) ] dx

To validate the method, two parabolic test functions were chosen: P(x) = 0.001x² + 20 and Q(x) = −0.001x² + 10, producing a diverging channel whose area grows with x.

With area known at every cross-section, continuity enforces conservation of mass flow:

Q = A₁V₁ = A₂V₂ → V(x) = Q / A(x)

Velocity at each slice is solved from the inlet flow rate and local area, then substituted into Bernoulli's equation to recover pressure:

P₁ + ½ρV₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρV₂² + ρgh₂

The domain is discretized into 100 cross-sectional slices (n = 0 to 99). At each slice the solver computes area, solves for velocity, then solves for pressure — iterating downstream until the full field is resolved.

2024 Bernoulli Flow Solver Python Project
Project — Python Implementation

A Python solver that takes any two user-defined wall functions and computes velocity and pressure at every cross-section along the channel. The solver discretizes the geometry into 100 slices, applies continuity to find velocity, then solves Bernoulli's equation downstream — outputting full velocity and pressure field plots with color gradients mapped to magnitude.
